The question that changes everything

Imagine your company runs a marketing campaign in March. Sales go up 20% that month. Did the campaign work?

Most analysts would say yes. But that’s incomplete. The right question is:

What would sales have been in March if we had never run the campaign?

That hypothetical — the world where the campaign didn’t happen — is a counterfactual. And constructing a credible counterfactual is the entire project of causal inference.

This distinction matters enormously. A 20% increase in sales could mean the campaign was transformative. Or it could mean sales were going to rise anyway — seasonality, a competitor’s exit, a product going viral — and the campaign did nothing. Without a counterfactual, you can’t tell.

The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: we can never directly observe a counterfactual.

Every unit — a person, a firm, a country — exists in only one state at a time. Either they received the treatment, or they didn’t. We observe one outcome. The other is forever hidden.

Holland (1986) called this the Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference. Formally, for unit i:

\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)

where:

  • Y_i(1) = outcome if treated
  • Y_i(0) = outcome if not treated
  • \tau_i = the individual causal effect

We observe either Y_i(1) or Y_i(0), never both. The individual effect \tau_i is fundamentally unobservable. This is sometimes called the missing data problem of causal inference.

Since we cannot observe both potential outcomes for the same unit, we have to construct a plausible counterfactual from other data. How we do that — and what assumptions we need — defines the entire field of causal inference.

Prediction vs. causal inference

Before going further, it’s worth being precise about what causal inference is not.

Prediction asks: given what I observe, what will happen next?
Causal inference asks: if I intervene and change X, what happens because of X?

A model can be excellent at prediction and completely useless for decision-making. The classic example: umbrella sales predict rain very well. But buying umbrellas doesn’t cause rain. If you’re a city planner deciding whether to build drainage infrastructure, “umbrella sales are high” is useful prediction but worthless causal knowledge.

Pearl (2009) formalises this with his do-calculus. Observational data gives us P(Y \mid X = x) — the distribution of outcomes given that we observe X = x. Causal inference targets P(Y \mid do(X = x)) — the distribution of outcomes if we were to set X = x by intervention. These are different objects, and confusing them is the source of most errors in data-driven decision-making.

The identification landscape: how we build counterfactuals

Since we can’t observe the counterfactual directly, every causal identification strategy is really a different answer to: which other data can stand in for the missing potential outcome?

Here are the main strategies, grouped by the type of data they exploit.

Building counterfactuals in practice

Let’s make this concrete. Consider a simple case: we observe a unit over time, an intervention happens at some point, and we want to estimate its effect. The counterfactual is the trajectory the unit would have followed had the intervention never happened.

The Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT) is the average gap between the observed and counterfactual trajectories in the post-treatment period. In the graph above, the ATT at time t is:

ATT(t) = E[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \mid D_i = 1]

The problem: we only observe one of the two lines. Every identification strategy is a different way of estimating the red dashed line.

Counterfactuals by data structure

The right strategy depends heavily on the structure of your data. Here I’ll sketch the three main settings — time series, panel data, and cross-sectional with covariates (the g-computation approach) — and show how each constructs the counterfactual.

Time-series counterfactuals

When you have a single unit observed over time (a country, a company, a region) and an intervention at a known date, the counterfactual is what the series would have looked like had the intervention never happened.

The key tool here is fitting a model to the pre-intervention period and extrapolating it into the post-period. This is the logic of CausalImpact (Brodersen et al., 2015), Causal ARIMA (Menchetti et al., 2022), and synthetic controls.

The credibility rests on the quality of the pre-intervention model fit and the absence of other events coinciding with the intervention.

Panel data counterfactuals: Difference-in-Differences

When you have multiple units observed over time, and only some are treated, you can use the untreated units to construct the counterfactual for the treated ones. This is the idea behind difference-in-differences (DiD).

The key assumption is parallel trends: in the absence of treatment, the treated and control groups would have followed the same trend. The counterfactual for the treated group after treatment is their pre-treatment level plus the trend observed in the control group.

The DiD estimator is:

\widehat{ATT} = \underbrace{(\bar{Y}_{treated,post} - \bar{Y}_{treated,pre})}_{\text{change in treated}} - \underbrace{(\bar{Y}_{control,post} - \bar{Y}_{control,pre})}_{\text{change in control}}

This subtracts the control group’s trend from the treated group’s change. Whatever is left — the part of the change not explained by the common trend — is the causal effect.

Cross-sectional counterfactuals: G-computation

When you have cross-sectional data with covariates and no natural control group or time series, you can still construct counterfactuals using a technique called G-computation (also called the g-formula or outcome regression approach).

The idea: fit a model E[Y \mid X, D] using observed data, then use it to predict what outcomes would have been under a hypothetical intervention — i.e., setting D = 0 for everyone, or D = 1 for everyone.

Under the assumption of no unmeasured confounding (conditional exchangeability), this gives a consistent estimate of the causal effect.

The Average Treatment Effect (ATE) under g-computation is:

\widehat{ATE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[ \hat{E}[Y \mid X_i, D=1] - \hat{E}[Y \mid X_i, D=0] \right]

This is remarkably powerful: it works for any outcome model, handles continuous and discrete treatments, and naturally extends to heterogeneous effects (by looking at the distribution of unit-level effects rather than just the mean).

The main vulnerability is model misspecification — if the outcome model is wrong, the counterfactual predictions are wrong. This is why doubly robust estimators combine g-computation with propensity score weighting: if either model is correctly specified, the estimate is consistent.

Connecting the pieces

Each of the three approaches above is really the same idea at different levels of abstraction:

  1. What would have happened to this unit had things been different?
  2. We construct the answer using different sources of variation — time-series trends, cross-unit comparisons, or covariate-adjusted predictions.
  3. The causal claim is only as credible as the assumption that our constructed counterfactual is a valid stand-in for the unobserved one.

```{r}
#| label: missing-cf
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-cap: "The missing counterfactual. For each unit we observe only one potential outcome. The causal effect — the gap between the two — is never directly observed."

library(tidyverse)
library(ggplot2)

set.seed(42)
n <- 10

df <- tibble(
  unit    = 1:n,
  y1      = round(50 + rnorm(n, 0, 8) + seq(0, 20, length.out = n)),
  y0      = round(40 + rnorm(n, 0, 8) + seq(0, 15, length.out = n)),
  treated = sample(c(TRUE, FALSE), n, replace = TRUE)
) |>
  mutate(
    y_observed = if_else(treated, y1, y0),
    y_missing  = if_else(treated, y0, y1)
  )

df_long <- bind_rows(
  df |> transmute(unit, y = y_observed, type = "Observed"),
  df |> transmute(unit, y = y_missing,  type = "Counterfactual (missing)")
)

ggplot(df_long, aes(x = unit, y = y, shape = type, colour = type)) +
  geom_point(size = 3.5) +
  geom_segment(
    data = df,
    aes(x = unit, xend = unit, y = y0, yend = y1),
    inherit.aes = FALSE,
    colour = "grey60", linetype = "dashed", linewidth = 0.5
  ) +
  scale_shape_manual(values = c("Observed" = 16, "Counterfactual (missing)" = 4)) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = c("Observed" = "#2c7bb6", "Counterfactual (missing)" = "#d7191c")) +
  labs(
    x = "Unit", y = "Outcome",
    colour = NULL, shape = NULL,
    title = "The missing counterfactual",
    subtitle = "For each unit we see only one potential outcome. The causal effect is the gap."
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(legend.position = "bottom")
```

## Building counterfactuals in practice

Let's make this concrete. Consider a simple case: we observe a unit over time, an intervention happens at some point, and we want to estimate its effect. The counterfactual is the trajectory the unit *would have followed* had the intervention never happened.

```{r}
#| label: att-trajectory
#| echo: false
#| fig-cap: "A counterfactual trajectory. We observe the treated unit up to the intervention, then two possible post-period paths: what actually happened, and what would have happened without treatment."

set.seed(1)
time <- 1:20
intervention_t <- 12

trend            <- 0.8 * time
noise            <- rnorm(20, 0, 2)
treatment_effect <- c(rep(0, intervention_t), cumsum(rep(1.2, 20 - intervention_t)))

y_observed       <- trend + noise + treatment_effect
y_counterfactual <- trend + noise

df_ts <- tibble(
  time,
  observed       = y_observed,
  counterfactual = y_counterfactual
) |>
  pivot_longer(-time, names_to = "series", values_to = "y")

ggplot(df_ts, aes(x = time, y = y, colour = series, linetype = series)) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = intervention_t + 0.5, linetype = "dotted", colour = "grey40") +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1) +
  geom_point(size = 2) +
  annotate("text", x = intervention_t + 0.8, y = max(y_observed) - 2,
           label = "Intervention", hjust = 0, size = 3.5, colour = "grey40") +
  annotate("segment",
           x = 17, xend = 17,
           y = y_observed[17], yend = y_counterfactual[17],
           arrow = arrow(ends = "both", length = unit(0.15, "cm")),
           colour = "#d7191c") +
  annotate("text", x = 17.3, y = (y_observed[17] + y_counterfactual[17]) / 2,
           label = "ATT", hjust = 0, size = 3.5, colour = "#d7191c") +
  scale_colour_manual(
    values = c("observed" = "#2c7bb6", "counterfactual" = "#d7191c"),
    labels = c("observed" = "Observed (treated)", "counterfactual" = "Counterfactual (never treated)")
  ) +
  scale_linetype_manual(
    values = c("observed" = "solid", "counterfactual" = "dashed"),
    labels = c("observed" = "Observed (treated)", "counterfactual" = "Counterfactual (never treated)")
  ) +
  labs(
    title    = "Observed vs. counterfactual trajectory",
    subtitle = "The causal effect (ATT) is the gap at each post-treatment period",
    x = "Time", y = "Outcome", colour = NULL, linetype = NULL
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(legend.position = "bottom")
```

```{r}
#| label: ts-arima-cf
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-cap: "Time-series counterfactual: fit a model on pre-treatment data, extrapolate, compare to observed."

library(forecast)

set.seed(7)
n_pre   <- 24
n_post  <- 12
n_total <- n_pre + n_post

y_pre      <- 100 + arima.sim(list(ar = 0.7), n = n_pre, sd = 3) |> as.numeric()
y_post_cf  <- tail(y_pre, 1) + arima.sim(list(ar = 0.7), n = n_post, sd = 3) |> as.numeric()
y_post_obs <- y_post_cf + seq(2, 18, length.out = n_post)

y_full    <- c(y_pre, y_post_obs)
time_full <- 1:n_total

fit <- auto.arima(ts(y_pre))
fc  <- forecast(fit, h = n_post)

df_ts2 <- tibble(
  time     = time_full,
  observed = y_full,
  cf_mean  = c(rep(NA, n_pre), fc$mean    |> as.numeric()),
  cf_lo    = c(rep(NA, n_pre), fc$lower[, 2] |> as.numeric()),
  cf_hi    = c(rep(NA, n_pre), fc$upper[, 2] |> as.numeric())
)

ggplot(df_ts2, aes(x = time)) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = n_pre + 0.5, linetype = "dotted", colour = "grey50") +
  geom_ribbon(aes(ymin = cf_lo, ymax = cf_hi), fill = "#d7191c", alpha = 0.15) +
  geom_line(aes(y = observed), colour = "#2c7bb6", linewidth = 1) +
  geom_line(aes(y = cf_mean),  colour = "#d7191c", linewidth = 1, linetype = "dashed") +
  annotate("text", x = n_pre + 1, y = max(y_full, na.rm = TRUE),
           label = "Intervention", hjust = 0, size = 3.5, colour = "grey40") +
  labs(
    title    = "Time-series counterfactual via ARIMA extrapolation",
    subtitle = "Blue = observed. Red dashed = ARIMA forecast from pre-period (counterfactual). Shaded = 95% CI.",
    x = "Time", y = "Outcome"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13)
```

### Panel data counterfactuals: Difference-in-Differences

When you have **multiple units observed over time**, and only some are treated, you can use the untreated units to construct the counterfactual for the treated ones. This is the idea behind **difference-in-differences (DiD)**.

The key assumption is **parallel trends**: in the absence of treatment, the treated and control groups would have followed the same trend. The counterfactual for the treated group after treatment is their pre-treatment level plus the trend observed in the control group.

```{r}
#| label: did-plot
#| echo: false
#| fig-cap: "Difference-in-differences. Under parallel trends, the control group's post-treatment trend tells us what the treated group would have done."

set.seed(3)
time_did <- c(2019, 2020, 2021, 2022)

treat_line <- tibble(
  time  = time_did,
  y     = c(50, 52, 54, 70),
  group = "Treated (observed)"
)

ctrl_line <- tibble(
  time  = time_did,
  y     = c(48, 50, 52, 54),
  group = "Control"
)

cf_line <- tibble(
  time  = c(2021, 2022),
  y     = c(54, 56),
  group = "Counterfactual (parallel trend)"
)

all_lines <- bind_rows(treat_line, ctrl_line, cf_line)

colours <- c(
  "Treated (observed)"              = "#2c7bb6",
  "Control"                         = "#1a9641",
  "Counterfactual (parallel trend)" = "#d7191c"
)
linetypes <- c(
  "Treated (observed)"              = "solid",
  "Control"                         = "solid",
  "Counterfactual (parallel trend)" = "dashed"
)

ggplot(all_lines, aes(x = time, y = y, colour = group, linetype = group)) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 2021.5, linetype = "dotted", colour = "grey50") +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1) +
  geom_point(size = 3) +
  annotate("segment",
           x = 2022.05, xend = 2022.05, y = 56, yend = 70,
           arrow = arrow(ends = "both", length = unit(0.15, "cm")),
           colour = "#d7191c") +
  annotate("text", x = 2022.1, y = 63,
           label = "DiD\nestimate", size = 3.5, colour = "#d7191c", hjust = 0) +
  annotate("text", x = 2021.6, y = 72,
           label = "Treatment\nstarts", size = 3.5, colour = "grey40", hjust = 0) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = colours) +
  scale_linetype_manual(values = linetypes) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = time_did) +
  labs(
    title    = "Difference-in-differences",
    subtitle = "The control group's trend is the counterfactual for the treated group",
    x = NULL, y = "Outcome", colour = NULL, linetype = NULL
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(legend.position = "bottom")
```

The DiD estimator is:

$$\widehat{ATT} = \underbrace{(\bar{Y}_{treated,post} - \bar{Y}_{treated,pre})}_{\text{change in treated}} - \underbrace{(\bar{Y}_{control,post} - \bar{Y}_{control,pre})}_{\text{change in control}}$$

### Cross-sectional counterfactuals: G-computation

When you have **cross-sectional data with covariates** and no natural control group or time series, you can still construct counterfactuals using a technique called **G-computation** (also called the g-formula or outcome regression approach).

The idea: fit a model $E[Y \mid X, D]$ using observed data, then use it to predict what outcomes *would have been* under a hypothetical intervention — i.e., setting $D = 0$ for everyone, or $D = 1$ for everyone.

Under the assumption of **no unmeasured confounding** (conditional exchangeability), this gives a consistent estimate of the causal effect.

```{r}
#| label: gcomp-plot
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-cap: "G-computation: predict counterfactual outcomes by setting treatment to 0 for all units, holding covariates fixed."

library(broom)

set.seed(99)
n_gc <- 300

age      <- rnorm(n_gc, 45, 10)
severity <- rnorm(n_gc, 5, 2)
p_treat  <- plogis(-1 + 0.4 * severity + 0.01 * age)
treated_gc <- rbinom(n_gc, 1, p_treat)
y_gc <- 30 - 3 * treated_gc + 0.5 * age + 2 * severity + rnorm(n_gc, 0, 5)

df_gc  <- tibble(y = y_gc, treated = treated_gc, age, severity)
fit_gc <- lm(y ~ treated + age + severity, data = df_gc)

y_pred_1 <- predict(fit_gc, newdata = df_gc |> mutate(treated = 1))
y_pred_0 <- predict(fit_gc, newdata = df_gc |> mutate(treated = 0))
ate_gc   <- mean(y_pred_1 - y_pred_0)

df_plot_gc <- tibble(
  unit     = 1:n_gc,
  y_treat1 = y_pred_1,
  y_treat0 = y_pred_0,
  effect   = y_pred_1 - y_pred_0
) |>
  arrange(effect) |>
  mutate(rank = row_number())

df_plot_long <- df_plot_gc |>
  pivot_longer(c(y_treat1, y_treat0), names_to = "scenario", values_to = "y_pred") |>
  mutate(scenario = if_else(scenario == "y_treat1", "D = 1 (treated world)", "D = 0 (control world)"))

ggplot(df_plot_long, aes(x = rank, y = y_pred, colour = scenario)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.5, size = 0.8) +
  geom_smooth(se = FALSE, linewidth = 1.2) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = mean(y_pred_1), linetype = "dashed", colour = "#2c7bb6", alpha = 0.5) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = mean(y_pred_0), linetype = "dashed", colour = "#d7191c", alpha = 0.5) +
  annotate("text", x = 280, y = mean(y_pred_1) + 1.5, label = "E[Y(1)]", colour = "#2c7bb6", size = 3.5) +
  annotate("text", x = 280, y = mean(y_pred_0) - 1.5, label = "E[Y(0)]", colour = "#d7191c", size = 3.5) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = c("D = 1 (treated world)" = "#2c7bb6", "D = 0 (control world)" = "#d7191c")) +
  labs(
    title    = "G-computation: predicted outcomes under each intervention",
    subtitle = glue::glue("Each unit's outcome is predicted under D=1 and D=0. ATE = {round(ate_gc, 2)}"),
    x = "Units (ranked by effect)", y = "Predicted outcome",
    colour = NULL
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(legend.position = "bottom")
```

The **Average Treatment Effect (ATE)** under g-computation is:

$$\widehat{ATE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[ \hat{E}[Y \mid X_i, D=1] - \hat{E}[Y \mid X_i, D=0] \right]$$

This is remarkably powerful: it works for any outcome model, handles continuous and discrete treatments, and naturally extends to heterogeneous effects (by looking at the distribution of unit-level effects rather than just the mean).

The main vulnerability is model misspecification — if the outcome model is wrong, the counterfactual predictions are wrong. This is why **doubly robust** estimators combine g-computation with propensity score weighting: if either model is correctly specified, the estimate is consistent.

## Connecting the pieces

Each of the three approaches above is really the same idea at different levels of abstraction:

1. **What would have happened** to this unit had things been different?
2. We construct the answer using different sources of variation — time-series trends, cross-unit comparisons, or covariate-adjusted predictions.
3. The causal claim is only as credible as the assumption that our constructed counterfactual is a valid stand-in for the unobserved one.
